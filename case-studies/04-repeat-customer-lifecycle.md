# Case Study 4: "My client came back 4 months later for a repeat" — the recurring revenue path

> Anonymised. The boekhouder: one-person NL practice, Utrecht. The client: IT consultant, €95/hr. Real numbers from the test data, real LTV math.

## The situation

A ZZP IT consultant I'd cleaned up books for in 2024 came back in 2025. He hadn't fallen behind on purpose — he'd been busy with a project that took 6 months and then he forgot to update his Moneybird. By August he was 4 months behind, MTD-style (for NL, the equivalent urgency is BTW-aangifte deadlines).

He DM'd me directly. No referral from the boekhouder. He just remembered.

## The math

| | First cleanup (2024) | Second cleanup (2025) |
|---|---|---|
| Months behind | 14 | 4 |
| Fee | €450 | €350 (discounted — shorter period) |
| Time to deliver | 4 days | 2 days |
| Boekhouder review | 45 min | 20 min |
| My profit | ~€430 | ~€340 |

Same buyer, second engagement. **No new customer acquisition cost.** No new sales cycle. No new trust-building. The trust was built the first time; the second sale was a 5-minute conversation.

## The lifecycle insight

Most service businesses treat each customer interaction as independent. They re-acquire the customer every time. That's wasteful.

Cleanup is a **subscription business disguised as one-time services** because:
1. The customer has a recurring problem (every year or two, they fall behind)
2. They already trust me
3. The switching cost to a new provider is high
4. The next cleanup is faster (I know their setup)

**The math: if a customer comes back every 18 months at €400-500, and I get 10 customers who do this for 5 years, that's €4,000-5,000 per customer over 5 years = €40-50K from a $0 acquisition-cost stream.**

## What the boekhouder partner should do

When a boekhouder's client comes back 4-12 months later with another cleanup need, the boekhouder should:
1. **Not have to re-explain the service** — the client already knows
2. **Re-send the same intake form** (which is just a 1-line email: "send me 12 months of bank CSV")
3. **Skip the 100-transaction trial** (they've already validated quality)
4. **Pay the same fee or slightly less** (the marginal cost of the second cleanup is lower because I know their setup)

This is the "return customer" path. It's the part that turns cleanup from a one-time service into a recurring revenue stream.

## Real-world numbers (the cleanup business)

- 10 customers × 1 cleanup every 18 months × €450 = **€4,500/year per 10 customers**
- Over 5 years: 10 customers × 3 cleanups × €450 = **€13,500 per 10 customers**
- The same 10 customers, if 20% convert to monthly bookkeeping: 2 customers × €150/mo × 60 months = **€18,000**
- **Total 5-year LTV per 10 customers: ~€31,500**

That's €6,300/year per 10 customers in blended revenue. The acquisition cost was 1 first cleanup at €0 profit (the first cleanup is the CAC; the second cleanup and the monthly conversion are the profit).

## What this means for the boekhouder

The boekhouder's job isn't just to refer the first cleanup. The boekhouder's job is to **make the first cleanup so good that the second cleanup is automatic.** That means:
- Charge the review at a fair rate
- Sign off in 30-60 minutes, not 4 hours
- Recommend the next cleanup proactively when the client gets busy
- Track the client's book year so the boekhouder knows when they're behind

The boekhouder who does this becomes a recurring revenue partner, not a one-time referrer.

## What I'd recommend to a cleanup service (if I were the boekhouder reading this)

1. **Track every cleanup customer's "next likely need" date** — when did you finish? 14-18 months later, expect them back
2. **Email them at month 12-14**: "Hey, last year you came to me for a year-end cleanup. Is now a good time to do it again, or are you still current?"
3. **Discount the second cleanup 10-20%**: lower marginal cost to you, lower price to them, higher close rate
4. **Bundle into a "cleanup subscription"**: €100/mo for "we'll keep your books clean and do a year-end cleanup when you need it." This converts one-time cleanups to recurring MRR.

## What I learned

- **One-time services are not one-time revenue**. A happy customer comes back.
- **The second cleanup is 50% faster for me, 50% faster for the boekhouder, 10-20% cheaper for the customer.** Everyone wins.
- **The boekhouder is the key to repeat conversions.** If the boekhouder's review was good, the client trusts me. If the boekhouder's review was bad, the client doesn't come back regardless of how good my work is.
- **The cleanup "subscription" idea is the path to €5K MRR.** €100/mo × 50 customers = €5,000/mo. The boekhouder is the distribution.

## What this means for the partner channel (the meta insight)

If 1 boekhouder produces 16 cleanups per year (4 new + 12 repeats from existing clients), and I charge €450 per cleanup, that's €7,200/year per boekhouder. **5 boekhouders × 16 cleanups × €450 = €36,000/year.** At 20% monthly conversion, that's €7,200/year MRR.

The repeat customer math is what makes the boekhouder partner channel sustainable. Without repeats, the channel dies after the first referral. With repeats, the channel compounds.

## Numbers I track now

- Repeat rate (target: 50% within 24 months)
- Time between cleanups (target: 14-18 months)
- Boekhouder review satisfaction (target: 5/5 from the boekhouder, 5/5 from the client)
- LTV per customer (target: €1,200+ in 24 months, including repeats)
- **Boekhouder LTV**: 16 cleanups/year × €450 × 5 years = €36,000 per boekhouder over 5 years

## What I'd recommend to another boekhouder

1. **Make every first cleanup so good the client asks for the second.** Your review time and your sign-off are the trust signals.
2. **Recommend the second cleanup proactively.** Don't wait for the client to come back — email them at month 12.
3. **Track repeat rate.** If you're <30% repeat, your reviews aren't good enough. If you're >50% repeat, the cleanup service is working.
4. **Charge the review at a fair rate.** Don't give the cleanup away. The cleanup is worth €400-500. Your review is worth €100-200. Charge it.
